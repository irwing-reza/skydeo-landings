# Shape SEO

Produce a decisive SEO brief without turning the work into an interview.

## 1. Inspect the ground truth

Read the content, page, repository, analytics, or brief already in scope. Reuse
facts the user supplied instead of asking them again. Research current search
results only when terminology or intent is materially uncertain, and keep that
research bounded.

## 2. Ask one intake question

Ask exactly one SEO question for the entire workflow, then wait once. Make it
easy to answer in prose:

> What should this page achieve in search? In one message, describe the ideal
> visitor and market, what they might search for, the action you want them to
> take, any proof or claims we can use, and any constraints—or say “use your
> best judgment.”

When the existing brief already answers some items, summarize those facts in
the question and ask only for corrections or missing context. Do not ask
follow-up SEO questions. Resolve remaining gaps with conservative professional
judgment and list consequential assumptions in the brief.

## 3. Deliver and apply the brief

Create one coherent recommendation covering:

- business outcome and primary measurement;
- audience, market, search job, and dominant intent;
- primary and supporting topics;
- distinctive promise and allowed evidence;
- SEO title, H1, meta description, canonical URL, and slug;
- outline, internal links, CTA, and measurement notes;
- category and reusable tags only when they create useful archives;
- overlap risks, assumptions, and deferred items.

Align the title, description, H1, content promise, and CTA to one intent. Treat
tags as site taxonomy, never meta keywords. Omit taxonomy for standalone pages.
After delivering the brief, continue implementation without requesting a
separate SEO approval unless the user asked to review it first.
